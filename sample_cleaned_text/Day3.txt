Analog Signal
•
An analog signal has infinitely many levels of
intensity over time. As the wave moves from value A
to value B, it passes through and includes an infinite
number of values along its path.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 4

--- Page 5 ---
Digital Signal
•
A digital signal, on the other hand, can have only a
limited number of defined values. Although each
value can be any number, it is often as simple as 1
and 0.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 5

Periodic and Nonperiodic
•
A periodic signal completes a pattern within a
measurable time frame, called a period, and repeats
that pattern over subsequent identical periods.
•
The completion of one full pattern is called a cycle.
•
A nonperiodic signal changes without exhibiting a
pattern or cycle that repeats over time. Both analog
and digital signals.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 6

Phase
•
The term phase, or phase shift, describes the position
of the waveform relative to time 0.
•
If we think of the wave as something that can be
shifted backward or forward along the time axis,
phase describes the amount of that shift. It indicates
the status of the first cycle.

Bandwidth
•
The range of frequencies contained in a composite
signal is its bandwidth. The bandwidth is normally a
difference between two numbers.
•
For example, if a composite signal contains
frequencies between 1000 and 5000, its bandwidth is
5000 − 1000, or 4000.

Bit Rate and Bit Length
•
The bit rate is the number of bits sent in 1s,
expressed in bits per second (bps).
•
The bit length is the distance one bit occupies on the
transmission medium.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 18

--- Page 19 ---
Transmission Impairment
•
Attenuation means a loss of energy. When a signal,
simple or composite, travels through a medium, it
loses some of its energy in overcoming the resistance
of the medium
•
Distortion means that the signal changes its form or
shape. Distortion can occur in a composite signal
made of different frequencies.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 19

--- Page 20 ---
Transmission Impairment
(Contd.)
•
Noise is another cause of impairment. Several types
of noise, such as thermal noise, induced noise,
crosstalk, and impulse noise, may corrupt the signal.
•
Thermal noise is the random motion of electrons in
a wire, which creates an extra signal not originally
sent by the transmitter.
•
Induced noise comes from sources such as motors.
•
Crosstalk is the effect of one wire on the other.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 20

Data Rate Limit
•
Nyquist Rate: For a noiseless channel, the Nyquist
bit rate formula defines the theoretical maximum bit
rate.
•
Shannon Capacity: The Shannon capacity
determines the theoretical highest data rate for a
noisy channel.
Computer Networks (CS 306), IIT Indore 21

Performance
•
Bandwidth: For performance, we evaluate the bandwidth in
terms of maximum bps that can be achieved
•
Throughput: The throughput is a measure of how fast we can
send data through a network.
•
Latency: The latency or delay defines how long it takes for an
entire message to completely arrive at the destination from the
time the first bit is sent out from the source.
